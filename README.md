# Approach

### Core Mindset Shift
Most "unintuitive" solutions feel weird at first because they rely on clever observations, optimizations, or patterns you haven't seen before (e.g., sliding window, monotonic stack, greedy choices that seem counter-intuitive).
Stop trying to memorize the full solution.
Focus on: Why does this work? What is the key insight? When should I reach for this?
### Step-by-Step Process for Every Problem (Especially Tricky Ones)

Attempt it yourself first (15–30 minutes)
Read the problem carefully. Try a brute-force approach on paper. Think about small examples.
This builds problem-solving muscles even if you fail.
When stuck, study the solution actively (don't just read)
Look at the editorial or a good video explanation (NeetCode is excellent for intuition).
Draw it out on paper/whiteboard. Visualize arrays, trees, pointers moving, states changing. Pictures help massively with intuition.
Ask "Why?" repeatedly:
Why does this reduce time complexity?
Why this data structure (hashmap vs set vs heap)?
What edge cases does it handle cleverly?

Explain the solution out loud in your own words as if teaching someone.

Implement it yourself without looking
Close the solution tab and code it from your understanding.
If you get stuck, peek only at the key insight, not the full code.
Force deeper understanding with these tricks
Read the solution in a different language than your main one (e.g., if you use Python, read Java solution). This prevents pattern-matching shortcuts and forces you to grasp the logic.
Break it into parts: Identify the pattern (sliding window? two pointers? DP state transition?).
Think of variations: How would I modify this for a slightly different constraint?

Review with spaced repetition (this is how you remember)
Don't solve once and forget. Use the forgetting curve to your advantage:
Re-solve the same problem after 1 day, then 3–4 days, then 1 week, then 2–3 weeks without looking at notes.
If you can reconstruct the approach quickly, great. If not, review the intuition again.
Keep a simple notebook or sheet: For each problem, write 1-sentence key insight + pattern name + when to use it. Review this list weekly.


### Best Daily Habits to Make It Stick

With your 2 LeetCode problems per day:
Spend more time on understanding than coding at the beginning. One easy + one medium is fine.
After solving, always add to your "patterns list".
Group by patterns, not random problems
Unintuitive solutions become intuitive when you see the same pattern 5–10 times.
Focus on one pattern at a time (e.g., spend a week on Sliding Window problems). Common high-impact patterns: Two Pointers, Sliding Window, Binary Search on Answer, Monotonic Stack, BFS/DFS for graphs, DP with states.
Active recall > passive reading
At the end of the day or week, pick 3–4 old problems and try to solve them from scratch. This strengthens memory far better than re-reading.
Explain or teach
Record a 2-minute voice note explaining the solution. Or explain it to an imaginary friend. Teaching exposes gaps in understanding.

### Realistic Expectations

First 1–2 months: Many solutions still feel unintuitive. That's normal.
After 3–6 months of consistent 2 problems/day + good review: You'll start spotting patterns faster, and "unintuitive" ones become "oh, this is X pattern with a twist."
The people who "get it" aren't smarter — they just reviewed and drew more.

### Extra Tips That Help a Lot

Use NeetCode 150 or similar curated lists — they focus on the most important patterns.
Watch explanations at 1.5x speed after trying yourself.
For very hard ones: Accept that some problems are "advanced" and move on after understanding. Come back later.
Track your "aha" moments: Note what made a tricky solution click for you.

The biggest mistake is rushing to the next problem without deeply processing why the solution works. Slow down on the "why" and "when", and the remembering will follow naturally through repetition.
